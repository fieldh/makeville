Class introduction

  • Plan is to focus more on techniques of wood joinery than to construct the complete project.

Joinery

Frame vs Carcase

  • Frame: miter joint
  • Carcase: mortise and tenon (traditional method) vs Festool Domino joiner (2007 - )
  • Dowels vs Biscuits vs Dominoes?

Wood Grain

  • End grain is super absorbent making and end grain-face grain glue joint extremely weak.
  • Miter joint (45 degree end grain to end grain like a picture frame) is thus weak so use a spline, which is a thin face grain insert. This is much stronger. The splines can vary in size and number.

Pre-Milling Preparation

Chalk Layout

  • Rough measurement of needed sizes marked onto the lumber in chalk
  • Try to place segments along flattest parts of the wood rather than simply in order. Work around damaged areas.
  • Leave about 5 inches extra on each piece since it will later be unusable due to compression from the jointer/planer process.

Miter/Chop Saw

  • Cut with face crown facing down and edge crown facing the back surface.
  • For a large piece of wood, cut in multiple iterations. The wood can start to rotate as it is cut, so multiple cuts allows you to reposition your hold.
  • A waterfall edge is one in which adjacent segments of wood are kept together so that the grain runs smoothly

Band Saw

  • Internal pressure test? Cut through and see if the two halves of wood align. If the wood is even, then it could be safe to do the entire milling process in one day, otherwise the wood needs to be left to sit for a week.
  • Reduce width of lumber so that it will fit through the jointer machine or so that it is close to its final width dimension.
  • If possible, cut through any division of plain vs rift sawn (pattern formed on wood depending on the location it was sawn from the tree.)

Milling Process

Jointer

  • Crown facing up so that at least two points of contact with the bottom face of the machine
  • Grain direction rising as the wood passes through the jointer
  • Result: bottom side is flat
  • Machine setting at 1⁄32 in.

Planer

  • Removes material from top of wood, making it parallel to bottom surface, which is flat from the jointer.
  • Grain direction falling as wood passes through planer (i.e. simply rotate the wood 180 degrees in the x-y plane when it comes out of the jointer.)
  • Machine setting at 1⁄32 in.
  • Goal is for all the boards to be the same thickness, so start planing the thickest boards and then decrease by 1⁄23 in width so that all the boards may be milled.
  • When finished planing the top surface, rotate 180 degrees in the xz plane to plane the other surface. This rotation will maintain the grain in the falling direction.
